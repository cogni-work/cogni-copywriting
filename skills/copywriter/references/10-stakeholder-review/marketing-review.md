# Marketing Review Criteria

## Quick Reference

- **Use when:** Document aims to influence, persuade, promote, or engage audiences
- **Core principle:** Persuasion requires audience resonance—speak to their motivations, not yours
- **Evaluation focus:** Audience resonance, persuasiveness, brand tone, call-to-action, emotional connection

## Perspective Philosophy

Marketing stakeholders prioritize audience engagement and persuasive impact. They need:
- **Audience-centric language** - Speak to audience motivations, not internal priorities
- **Persuasive structure** - Benefits before features, outcomes before process
- **Brand consistency** - Tone aligns with organizational voice
- **Clear call-to-action** - Explicit next steps
- **Emotional connection** - Tap into audience values and aspirations

**Anti-patterns to flag:**
- Inside-out thinking (what we offer vs. what audience gets)
- Feature dumping without benefit translation
- Generic, corporate-speak tone
- Weak or missing call-to-action
- Purely rational appeals without emotional hooks

## Evaluation Checklist

### 1. Audience Resonance (Weight: 30%)

**Question:** Does the document speak to audience motivations, pain points, and aspirations?

- ✅ **PASS:** Language consistently addresses audience perspective with "you" focus
  - Example: "You'll reduce customer churn 15%, freeing your team to focus on growth initiatives instead of firefighting retention issues"
  - Includes: audience pain points, specific benefits, aspirational outcomes
  - "You" vs. "we" ratio: 3:1 or higher
- ⚠️ **CONCERN:** Some audience focus but frequent inside-out language
  - Example: "Our CRM system has advanced features that improve efficiency" (feature-focused, vague benefit)
  - "You" vs. "we" ratio: 1:1
- ❌ **FAIL:** Primarily organization-focused language
  - Example: "We are proud to offer a comprehensive solution that leverages cutting-edge technology"
  - Inside-out language dominates

**Why this matters:** People don't care what you offer—they care what they get.

**Transformation pattern:**
- **Before:** "We offer a comprehensive CRM platform"
- **After:** "You get a complete view of every customer interaction in one dashboard"

---

### 2. Persuasiveness (Weight: 25%)

**Question:** Does the document follow persuasive structure (benefits before features, outcomes before process)?

- ✅ **PASS:** Benefits-driven structure with clear value proposition up front
  - Example: "**Reduce churn 15% and free 20 hours/week** (benefit) by automating follow-ups and centralizing customer data (feature/process)"
  - Framework alignment: FAB (Features-Advantages-Benefits) or PSB (Problem-Solution-Benefit)
  - Power words present: proven, validated, transform, accelerate
- ⚠️ **CONCERN:** Some benefits highlighted but feature-heavy sections present
  - Example: Leads with benefit but then describes system architecture before returning to outcomes
  - Power words sparse or generic
- ❌ **FAIL:** Feature-first structure with benefits buried or implied
  - Example: "Our system uses PostgreSQL 14 with Redis caching and Kubernetes deployment" (no benefit)
  - No clear value proposition

**Why this matters:** Features don't persuade—benefits do. People buy outcomes, not capabilities.

**Persuasive hierarchy:**
1. **Outcome** (what success looks like)
2. **Benefit** (what they gain)
3. **Advantage** (why this approach works)
4. **Feature** (what the solution includes)

---

### 3. Brand Tone (Weight: 20%)

**Question:** Does the tone align with organizational brand (professional, approachable, innovative, authoritative)?

- ✅ **PASS:** Tone consistently matches brand guidelines
  - Professional: clear, concise, credible, no jargon
  - Approachable: conversational but not casual, empathetic
  - Innovative: forward-looking, aspirational, confident
  - Authoritative: definitive (with hedging where appropriate), data-driven
- ⚠️ **CONCERN:** Tone mostly aligned but occasional inconsistencies
  - Example: Shifts from professional to overly casual mid-document
  - Example: Brand is innovative but language is conservative/generic
- ❌ **FAIL:** Tone significantly misaligned with brand
  - Example: Stuffy corporate-speak for an innovative startup brand
  - Example: Overly casual language for an enterprise B2B context

**Why this matters:** Brand consistency builds trust and recognition.

**Tone indicators:**
- **Professional:** "We recommend", "Based on analysis", "Industry-standard approach"
- **Approachable:** "Here's what this means for you", "Simple as that", "Let's break this down"
- **Innovative:** "Reimagine", "Transform", "Next-generation"
- **Authoritative:** "Proven approach", "Validated by research", "Industry benchmark"

---

### 4. Call-to-Action (Weight: 15%)

**Question:** Is there a clear, specific, and compelling call-to-action?

- ✅ **PASS:** Explicit CTA with specific action, deadline, and compelling reason
  - Example: "**Approve by March 15** to launch before Q2 peak season and capture $200K revenue opportunity"
  - Includes: verb (approve), deadline (March 15), reason (revenue opportunity)
  - CTA placement: early (executive summary) and late (conclusion)
- ⚠️ **CONCERN:** CTA present but vague or weak
  - Example: "Please review and provide feedback" (no urgency, unclear action)
  - Example: "Approve by end of quarter" (vague timeline)
- ❌ **FAIL:** No CTA or buried in conclusion
  - Example: "Thank you for your consideration" (passive)
  - Example: CTA only appears at end, not reinforced

**Why this matters:** Without a clear CTA, audiences don't know what to do next—inaction becomes the default.

**Strong CTA formula:**
- **Action verb** (Approve, Schedule, Sign, Commit)
- **Specificity** (what exactly to do)
- **Urgency** (when/why now)
- **Benefit** (what happens when they act)

---

### 5. Emotional Connection (Weight: 10%)

**Question:** Does the document tap into audience emotions, values, or aspirations beyond pure logic?

- ✅ **PASS:** Emotional appeals balanced with rational arguments
  - Example: "Imagine your team spending time building relationships instead of chasing down lost customer data—**that's the freedom this system delivers**"
  - Includes: aspirational language, relatable scenarios, value alignment
  - Emotional hooks: freedom, control, growth, security, innovation
- ⚠️ **CONCERN:** Some emotional language but primarily rational/transactional
  - Example: "This will improve efficiency" (rational but no emotional hook)
  - Minimal aspirational or values-driven language
- ❌ **FAIL:** Purely rational, transactional, no emotional resonance
  - Example: Document reads like technical specification
  - No connection to audience values or aspirations

**Why this matters:** Decisions are emotional, then rationalized. Pure logic rarely persuades.

**Emotional triggers (use appropriately for audience):**
- **Aspiration:** Transform, achieve, lead, pioneer
- **Security:** Protect, safeguard, proven, reliable
- **Control:** Empower, enable, freedom, autonomy
- **Belonging:** Join, partner, collaborate
- **Innovation:** Next-generation, cutting-edge, future-proof

## Scoring Guidelines

**Calculation:**
1. Evaluate each criterion: PASS (100), CONCERN (60), FAIL (0)
2. Multiply by criterion weight:
   - Audience Resonance: score × 0.30
   - Persuasiveness: score × 0.25
   - Brand Tone: score × 0.20
   - Call-to-Action: score × 0.15
   - Emotional Connection: score × 0.10
3. Sum weighted scores for total (0-100)

**Thresholds:**
- 85-100: Excellent, highly persuasive
- 70-84: Good, minor persuasive gaps
- 50-69: Concerns, weak persuasion
- 0-49: Failing, ineffective messaging

**Example calculation:**
- Audience Resonance: CONCERN (60 × 0.30 = 18)
- Persuasiveness: PASS (100 × 0.25 = 25)
- Brand Tone: PASS (100 × 0.20 = 20)
- Call-to-Action: CONCERN (60 × 0.15 = 9)
- Emotional Connection: PASS (100 × 0.10 = 10)
- **Total: 82** (Good)

## Common Improvement Patterns

### Pattern 1: Flip Inside-Out Language
**Before:** "We provide an advanced CRM solution with comprehensive features."
**After:** "You get complete customer visibility in one dashboard—no more hunting across spreadsheets."

### Pattern 2: Benefits Before Features
**Before:** "Our system uses machine learning algorithms to process customer data."
**After:** "Cut customer onboarding time 40% (benefit) with automated data enrichment (feature)."

### Pattern 3: Strengthen Call-to-Action
**Before:** "Please consider this proposal."
**After:** "Approve by March 15 to deploy before Q2 peak season and capture $200K revenue opportunity."

### Pattern 4: Add Emotional Hook
**Before:** "This system improves efficiency."
**After:** "Imagine your team building relationships instead of fighting spreadsheets—that's the freedom this delivers."

## Conflict Resolution

When marketing perspective conflicts with other stakeholders:

| Conflict | Resolution |
|----------|------------|
| **Executive wants brevity, Marketing wants storytelling** | Lead with brief ask, add compelling narrative in body |
| **Technical wants precision, Marketing wants emotional appeal** | Technical accuracy + power words (validated, proven, cutting-edge) |
| **Legal wants hedging, Marketing wants bold claims** | Confident but hedged: "Designed to deliver" vs. "Guarantees" |
| **End-user wants simplicity, Marketing wants sophistication** | Simple language with aspirational framing |

**Tiebreaker:** If deliverable type is marketing/sales material, marketing perspective takes priority.
