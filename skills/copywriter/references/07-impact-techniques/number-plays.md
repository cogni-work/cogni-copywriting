# Number Plays

## Quick Reference

**Use when:** Presenting data, results, benefits, or comparisons
**Core principle:** Real numbers create tangible understanding; percentages remain abstract
**Key techniques:** Ratio framing, comparative anchoring, specific quantification
**Target:** Transform every vague claim into concrete, memorable data

## Why Numbers Matter

Research shows that people respond more strongly to real, whole numbers than percentages because:
- Percentages create abstract pie charts in the mind
- Real numbers create tangible, relatable scenarios
- Specific figures signal credibility and research
- Comparative framing activates loss aversion

## Technique 1: Ratio Framing

Convert percentages to ratios for emotional impact.

| Abstract (Weak) | Concrete (Strong) |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| 12% of women | 1 in 8 women |
| 25% of projects fail | 1 in 4 projects fail |
| 80% success rate | 4 out of 5 succeed |
| 3% conversion | 3 buyers per 100 visitors |

**Pattern:** Percentages → "X in Y" or "X out of Y"

**Example:**
- ❌ "Our solution has a 95% satisfaction rate"
- ✅ "19 out of 20 customers recommend us"

## Technique 2: Specific Quantification

Replace vague quantifiers with precise numbers.

| Vague (Weak) | Specific (Strong) |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Many customers | 2,847 customers |
| Significant savings | $127,000 in savings |
| Soon | Within 14 days |
| Large team | 47 engineers |
| Most users | 89% of users |

**Pattern:** Abstract quantifier → Concrete number + unit

**Example:**
- ❌ "We've helped many companies improve efficiency"
- ✅ "We've helped 127 companies save an average of 340 hours per quarter"

## Technique 3: Comparative Anchoring

Frame numbers relative to familiar references.

### Time Comparisons
- ❌ "Saves 480 hours annually"
- ✅ "Saves 12 weeks per year—that's a full quarter back"
- ✅ "Gives your team 2 hours back every day"

### Money Comparisons
- ❌ "$36,000 annual cost"
- ✅ "$100 per day—less than a contractor's hourly rate"
- ✅ "About the cost of one mid-level hire"

### Scale Comparisons
- ❌ "1 million data points processed"
- ✅ "Processes 1 million records—equivalent to analyzing every transaction your company has ever made"

**Pattern:** Raw number → Familiar reference frame

## Technique 4: Before/After Contrast

Pair numbers to show transformation.

**Format:**
```
[Metric]: [Before Value] → [After Value] ([Improvement])
```

**Examples:**
- Processing time: 5 days → 4 hours (97% faster)
- Error rate: 12% → 0.3% (40x improvement)
- Customer response: 48 hours → 15 minutes (192x faster)
- Cost per unit: $45 → $12 (73% reduction)

**Table Format:**
| Metric | Before | After | Impact |
|--------|--------|-------|--------|
| Cycle time | 2 weeks | 2 days | 86% faster |
| Defect rate | 8% | 0.5% | 16x better |
| Cost | $500K | $200K | $300K saved |

## Technique 5: Compound Impact

Stack multiple numbers for cumulative effect.

**Single Number (Good):**
> "Our solution saves 10 hours per week."

**Compound Impact (Better):**
> "Our solution saves 10 hours per week. That's 520 hours per year—equivalent to 13 work weeks. For a team of 5, that's 2,600 hours annually, valued at $130,000 in recovered productivity."

**Pattern:** Unit impact → Extended timeframe → Team multiplier → Dollar value

## Technique 6: Rule of Three Numbers

Present three data points for memorability and completeness.

**Examples:**
- "40% faster, 60% cheaper, 100% reliable"
- "3 weeks to implement, 5 hours of training, 10x ROI"
- "$50K saved, 200 hours recovered, 95% satisfaction"

**Pattern:** Pick three complementary metrics that tell a complete story

## Technique 7: Precision Signals Credibility

Specific numbers feel researched; round numbers feel estimated.

| Feels Estimated | Feels Researched |
|-----------------|------------------|
| About 50% | 47.3% |
| Around $100K | $98,400 |
| Roughly 1,000 | 1,247 |
| Nearly 90% | 87% |

**When to use precision:**
- Data from actual measurements
- Financial projections
- Performance metrics

**When round numbers work:**
- Projections or estimates
- Order-of-magnitude comparisons
- When precision would mislead

## Application by Deliverable

### Memos & Emails
- Lead with the most impactful number
- Use 1-2 compound impact chains
- Ratio framing for risk/benefit

### Proposals
- Before/after contrasts in every section
- Compound ROI calculations
- Three-number summaries for executive review

### Reports
- Precise figures with methodology
- Tables for multi-metric comparisons
- Trend data with percentage changes

### One-Pagers
- Single hero number at top
- Three supporting metrics
- Comparative anchor for context

## Number Plays Checklist

Before finalizing, verify:

- [ ] Every "many/most/significant" replaced with specific number
- [ ] Key percentages converted to ratios where more impactful
- [ ] At least one before/after contrast included
- [ ] Numbers anchored to familiar reference frames
- [ ] Compound impact calculated for headline metrics
- [ ] Three-number rule applied to key benefits
- [ ] Precision level appropriate (estimates rounded, data precise)

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

❌ **Number overload:** Too many statistics dilute impact
❌ **Unsourced precision:** "47.3%" without data source feels manipulative
❌ **Irrelevant metrics:** Numbers that don't connect to reader's concerns
❌ **Scale mismatch:** Comparing millions to thousands without context
❌ **Time period mixing:** Annual vs. monthly vs. daily without clarity
