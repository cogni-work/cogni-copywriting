# Plain Language Principles

## Quick Reference
**Use when:** All deliverable types, especially customer-facing or broad audience documents
**Core principle:** Write so anyone can understand on first reading
**Key techniques:** Common words, short sentences, reader-focused organization
**Standard:** 8th-10th grade reading level for general business writing

## Overview

Plain language is communication your audience can understand the first time they read or hear it. It's not "dumbing down"—it's removing unnecessary barriers between your message and your reader.

**Benefits:**
- Reduces misunderstandings and errors
- Saves reader time (and therefore costs)
- Increases compliance and completion rates
- Improves accessibility for non-native speakers
- Enhances credibility through clarity

## Core Principles

### 1. Use Common, Everyday Words

Choose words most people know. Avoid jargon, formal language, and technical terms unless your audience requires them.

❌ **Avoid:** "facilitate," "utilize," "implement," "leverage," "optimize"
✅ **Better:** "help," "use," "start" or "do," "use," "improve"

❌ **Avoid:** "We will utilize the new system to optimize operational efficiencies."
✅ **Better:** "We will use the new system to improve how we work."

**Word Choice Guide:**

| Instead of | Use |
|------------|-----|
| ascertain | find out, learn |
| commence | start, begin |
| endeavor | try |
| facilitate | help, make easier |
| implement | carry out, start, do |
| leverage | use, apply |
| necessitate | require, need |
| obtain | get |
| optimum | best |
| prioritize | rank, put first |
| purchase | buy |
| terminate | end, stop |
| utilize | use |

### 2. Write Short Sentences and Paragraphs

**Sentences:** Aim for 15-20 words average. Break complex ideas into multiple short sentences.

❌ **Avoid:** "To improve customer satisfaction scores, which have declined 12% over the past quarter due to longer wait times and inconsistent service quality, we will implement a new training program for all customer service representatives that focuses on both technical knowledge and communication skills." (47 words)

✅ **Better:** "Customer satisfaction scores dropped 12% last quarter. Wait times increased and service quality became inconsistent. We will implement a new training program to fix this. The program covers technical knowledge and communication skills." (35 words in 4 sentences = 8.75 words per sentence)

**Paragraphs:** Target 3-5 sentences per paragraph. One main idea per paragraph.

### 3. Use Active Voice

Active voice makes clear who does what. It's more direct and engaging than passive voice.

❌ **Avoid:** "The decision was made by the committee."
✅ **Better:** "The committee decided."

❌ **Avoid:** "Your application will be reviewed by our team within 5 business days."
✅ **Better:** "Our team will review your application within 5 business days."

**Exception:** Use passive voice when:
- The actor is unknown: "The building was damaged"
- The actor doesn't matter: "Spanish is spoken here"
- You want to emphasize the action over the actor: "The policy was approved"

### 4. Address the Reader Directly

Use "you" and "your" to speak directly to readers. Use "we" for your organization. Avoid third person ("customers must" → "you must").

❌ **Avoid:** "Employees should submit timesheets by Friday."
✅ **Better:** "Submit your timesheet by Friday."

❌ **Avoid:** "The customer will receive a confirmation email."
✅ **Better:** "You will receive a confirmation email."

### 5. Use Verbs, Not Nominalizations

Nominalizations turn verbs into nouns, making writing stuffy and passive.

| Nominalization | Strong Verb |
|----------------|-------------|
| make a decision | decide |
| conduct an investigation | investigate |
| provide assistance | assist, help |
| give consideration to | consider |
| reach a conclusion | conclude |
| make an improvement | improve |
| perform an analysis | analyze |

❌ **Avoid:** "We will conduct an investigation into the matter."
✅ **Better:** "We will investigate the matter."

❌ **Avoid:** "The committee will give consideration to your proposal."
✅ **Better:** "The committee will consider your proposal."

### 6. Remove Redundancies and Filler

Many common phrases add words without adding meaning.

**Redundant Phrases:**
- "advance planning" → "planning" (all planning is in advance)
- "end result" → "result"
- "final outcome" → "outcome"
- "past experience" → "experience"
- "completely eliminate" → "eliminate"
- "close proximity" → "near"

**Wordy Phrases to Replace:**

| Wordy | Concise |
|-------|---------|
| at this point in time | now |
| due to the fact that | because |
| in order to | to |
| with regard to | about |
| in the event that | if |
| for the purpose of | to, for |
| a number of | some, several, many |
| in the near future | soon |

### 7. Organize for Your Reader

Put the most important information first. Use logical structure with clear headers.

**Inverted pyramid approach:**
1. Main point or conclusion
2. Supporting details
3. Background information

**Use descriptive headers:**
❌ **Avoid:** "Background," "Discussion," "Analysis"
✅ **Better:** "Why Costs Increased 40%," "Three Options to Reduce Spending," "Recommended Solution"

### 8. Define Technical Terms

When you must use technical terms, define them on first use.

❌ **Avoid:** "We need to improve our CSAT and NPS scores."
✅ **Better:** "We need to improve our customer satisfaction (CSAT) and Net Promoter Score (NPS), which measure how happy customers are with our service."

**Approaches:**
- **Inline definition:** "We'll use APIs (application programming interfaces) to connect the systems."
- **Parenthetical:** "The SLA (Service Level Agreement) guarantees 99.9% uptime."
- **Glossary:** For documents with many terms, add a glossary section

## Quality Checks

✓ **Grade level:** Target 8th-10th grade for general business (use readability tools)
✓ **Word choice:** Prefer words of one or two syllables when possible
✓ **Sentence length:** Average 15-20 words
✓ **Paragraph length:** 3-5 sentences
✓ **Active voice:** 80% or more
✓ **Personal pronouns:** "You" and "we" appear throughout
✓ **Jargon:** All technical terms defined or eliminated
✓ **Test:** Can a stranger understand on first reading?

## Common Resistance and Responses

**"Plain language sounds unprofessional."**
Reality: Clear writing builds credibility. Unclear writing frustrates readers and wastes time.

**"My audience is technical and expects technical language."**
Reality: Even technical audiences appreciate clarity. You can be technical and clear simultaneously.

**"Plain language means oversimplifying complex topics."**
Reality: Plain language means explaining complexity clearly, not removing necessary detail.

## Examples by Deliverable Type

### Business Letter
❌ **Before:** "Pursuant to our recent communication, we are unable to acquiesce to your request for a refund due to the fact that the product was utilized beyond the warranty period."

✅ **After:** "We cannot give you a refund because you used the product after the warranty expired. We discussed this in our last email."

### Technical Email
❌ **Before:** "The implementation of the new authentication protocol will necessitate reconfiguration of client-side certificates."

✅ **After:** "You'll need to reconfigure your security certificates to use the new login system."

### Policy Document
❌ **Before:** "Employees are required to obtain supervisory approval prior to the commencement of any travel for business purposes."

✅ **After:** "Get your supervisor's approval before you travel for work."
