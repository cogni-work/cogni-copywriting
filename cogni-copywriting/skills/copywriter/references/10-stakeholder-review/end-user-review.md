# End-User Review Criteria

## Quick Reference

- **Use when:** Document targets general audiences, customers, non-specialists, or end-users of a product/service
- **Core principle:** Clarity above all—if the average reader can't understand it, it fails
- **Evaluation focus:** Accessibility, plain language, actionability, visual clarity, empathy

## Perspective Philosophy

End-user stakeholders represent the general audience without specialized knowledge. They need:
- **Plain language** - No jargon, acronyms, or insider terminology
- **Immediate clarity** - Understand the point in seconds
- **Actionable guidance** - Clear next steps
- **Visual simplicity** - Easy to scan and navigate
- **Empathetic tone** - Respectful, not condescending

**Anti-patterns to flag:**
- Unexplained jargon or acronyms
- Dense paragraphs requiring slow reading
- Missing or vague action steps
- Talking down to the reader
- Assuming insider knowledge

## Evaluation Checklist

### 1. Plain Language (Weight: 30%)

**Question:** Is the document written in simple, everyday language without unexplained jargon?

- ✅ **PASS:** Plain language throughout, technical terms defined on first use
  - Example: "Customer Relationship Management (CRM) system—a tool that helps track every customer interaction in one place"
  - Flesch Reading Ease: 50-60 (conversational)
  - Short words preferred: "use" not "utilize", "help" not "facilitate"
  - Acronyms spelled out on first use
- ⚠️ **CONCERN:** Mostly plain language but occasional undefined jargon
  - Example: "The CRM system integrates with your tech stack" (what's a tech stack?)
  - Some complex terminology without definitions
  - Flesch score: 40-50 (somewhat difficult)
- ❌ **FAIL:** Heavy jargon, undefined acronyms, complex terminology
  - Example: "Leverage synergistic paradigms to optimize downstream workflows"
  - Multiple undefined acronyms (API, SDK, SaaS, etc.)
  - Flesch score: <40 (difficult)

**Why this matters:** If readers can't understand it, they can't act on it.

**Plain language checklist:**
- ✓ Short, common words (use not utilize, help not facilitate)
- ✓ Active voice (We recommend vs. It is recommended)
- ✓ Short sentences (15-20 words average)
- ✓ Acronyms spelled out on first use
- ✓ Technical terms defined in context

---

### 2. Immediate Clarity (Weight: 25%)

**Question:** Can a general reader grasp the main point in the first 30 seconds?

- ✅ **PASS:** Main point crystal clear in first paragraph, reinforced visually
  - Example: "**We're switching to a new customer tracking system on March 1.** This means faster responses to your questions and better service."
  - First paragraph = complete summary
  - Key point bolded or emphasized
  - Scannable structure (bullets, short paragraphs)
- ⚠️ **CONCERN:** Main point stated but requires reading multiple paragraphs to understand
  - Example: First paragraph mentions "changes coming" but doesn't specify what or when
  - Important details buried in body text
- ❌ **FAIL:** Main point unclear or buried deep in document
  - Example: Opens with background/context before stating purpose
  - Requires full document read to understand what's being asked

**Why this matters:** General audiences scan, they don't study. Buried points get missed.

**Immediate clarity test:**
- After reading only the first paragraph, can the reader answer:
  - What is this about?
  - Why does it matter to me?
  - What (if anything) do I need to do?

---

### 3. Actionability (Weight: 20%)

**Question:** Are next steps crystal clear with specific, concrete instructions?

- ✅ **PASS:** Step-by-step instructions with no ambiguity
  - Example:
    ```
    **What you need to do:**
    1. Review your account by Feb 28
    2. Click "Confirm" in the email we sent
    3. Call 1-800-555-0100 if you have questions
    ```
  - Includes: numbered steps, specific actions, deadlines, contact info
  - Optional vs. required actions clearly distinguished
- ⚠️ **CONCERN:** Actions stated but vague or missing key details
  - Example: "Please update your preferences" (how? by when?)
  - Example: "Contact us if you have questions" (how to contact?)
- ❌ **FAIL:** No clear action steps or what to do next is implied
  - Example: "We encourage you to familiarize yourself with the new system" (how?)
  - Example: Document ends without telling reader what to do

**Why this matters:** Vague instructions lead to inaction or incorrect action.

**Action clarity checklist:**
- ✓ Specific verb (Review, Click, Call, not "Consider" or "Familiarize")
- ✓ Deadline (by Feb 28, not "soon")
- ✓ How to do it (click link, call number, visit website)
- ✓ Who to contact with questions (name, email, phone)

---

### 4. Visual Clarity (Weight: 15%)

**Question:** Is the document easy to scan with clear visual hierarchy and formatting?

- ✅ **PASS:** Excellent visual structure optimized for scanning
  - Clear headers (H1, H2, H3 hierarchy)
  - Short paragraphs (3-4 lines max)
  - Bullets for lists (not comma-separated in prose)
  - Key info bolded or in callout boxes
  - White space between sections
- ⚠️ **CONCERN:** Some visual structure but dense sections present
  - Example: Headers used but paragraphs exceed 5 lines
  - Example: Lists embedded in paragraphs instead of bulleted
- ❌ **FAIL:** Wall of text with poor visual hierarchy
  - Example: Long paragraphs (7+ lines) with no breaks
  - Example: No bullets, limited headers, everything looks the same
  - Example: Key info buried in paragraph 4

**Why this matters:** General audiences scan before reading. Poor visual design means key info gets missed.

**Visual clarity checklist:**
- ✓ Headers every 3-4 paragraphs
- ✓ Paragraphs ≤4 lines
- ✓ Bullets for 3+ items
- ✓ Bold for key numbers/dates
- ✓ White space between sections

---

### 5. Empathy & Tone (Weight: 10%)

**Question:** Is the tone respectful, helpful, and appropriate for a general audience?

- ✅ **PASS:** Warm, helpful tone without condescension
  - Example: "Here's what's changing and what it means for you" (respectful, clear)
  - Example: "We know changes can be confusing—we're here to help" (empathetic)
  - Conversational but professional
  - "You" language (not "users" or "customers")
- ⚠️ **CONCERN:** Professional but impersonal or occasionally condescending
  - Example: "Users must familiarize themselves with new procedures" (impersonal, demanding)
  - Example: Overly formal or bureaucratic language
- ❌ **FAIL:** Condescending, dismissive, or overly complex tone
  - Example: "As any informed user would know..." (condescending)
  - Example: "Failure to comply will result in..." (threatening)
  - Example: Dense legalese without plain language alternative

**Why this matters:** Tone affects whether readers trust and engage with the message.

**Empathetic tone indicators:**
- ✓ "You" and "your" (not "the user")
- ✓ Helpful language: "Here's how", "We're here to help"
- ✓ Acknowledgment: "We know this is a change"
- ✓ Conversational: "Here's what's happening" not "Users are hereby notified"

## Scoring Guidelines

**Calculation:**
1. Evaluate each criterion: PASS (100), CONCERN (60), FAIL (0)
2. Multiply by criterion weight:
   - Plain Language: score × 0.30
   - Immediate Clarity: score × 0.25
   - Actionability: score × 0.20
   - Visual Clarity: score × 0.15
   - Empathy & Tone: score × 0.10
3. Sum weighted scores for total (0-100)

**Thresholds:**
- 85-100: Excellent, highly accessible
- 70-84: Good, minor clarity gaps
- 50-69: Concerns, accessibility issues
- 0-49: Failing, not user-friendly

**Example calculation:**
- Plain Language: PASS (100 × 0.30 = 30)
- Immediate Clarity: CONCERN (60 × 0.25 = 15)
- Actionability: PASS (100 × 0.20 = 20)
- Visual Clarity: CONCERN (60 × 0.15 = 9)
- Empathy & Tone: PASS (100 × 0.10 = 10)
- **Total: 84** (Good)

## Common Improvement Patterns

### Pattern 1: Simplify Language
**Before:** "Utilize the integrated CRM solution to facilitate customer engagement optimization."
**After:** "Use the new customer system to respond to customers faster."

### Pattern 2: Front-Load Clarity
**Before:** (Paragraph 1: background, Paragraph 2: context, Paragraph 3: main point)
**After:** **"We're switching to a new customer system on March 1."** (First sentence, first paragraph)

### Pattern 3: Make Actions Specific
**Before:** "Please familiarize yourself with the new system at your earliest convenience."
**After:**
```
**What you need to do:**
1. Watch the 3-minute tutorial at [link] by Feb 28
2. Try logging in to test your access
3. Call 1-800-555-0100 if you need help
```

### Pattern 4: Improve Visual Clarity
**Before:** (8-line paragraph with embedded list)
**After:**
```
**Key benefits:**
- Faster responses (24 hours → 4 hours)
- Complete history in one place
- Mobile access anywhere
```

## Conflict Resolution

When end-user perspective conflicts with other stakeholders:

| Conflict | Resolution |
|----------|------------|
| **Executive wants concise, End-user wants detailed** | Executive summary + detailed FAQ section |
| **Technical wants precision, End-user wants simplicity** | Plain language with technical glossary |
| **Legal wants disclaimers, End-user wants clarity** | Main message in plain language + disclaimer in separate section |
| **Marketing wants sophistication, End-user wants simple** | Simple language with aspirational framing |

**Tiebreaker:** If document audience is explicitly "general public" or "customers," end-user perspective takes priority.
